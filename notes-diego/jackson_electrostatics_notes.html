1. The Core Frame

Electrostatics as a Boundary-Value Theory

The single most useful mindset from Jackson is that the differential equation is only half the problem. The source tells me what is happening locally; the boundary conditions tell me which solution is physically allowed.

Starting point

∇ · E = ρ / ε0,    ∇ × E = 0,    E = -∇φ
∇2φ = -ρ / ε0

This is Poisson's equation. In any region where ρ = 0, it reduces to Laplace's equation:

∇2φ = 0

That looks innocent, but it is the engine behind uniqueness, separation of variables, Green's functions, and the method of images.

What I should always ask first

  • Where is the charge, and where is the charge-free region?
  • Am I solving Poisson or Laplace in the region of interest?
  • What boundary data are fixed: φ, ∂φ/∂n, or mixed?
  • Is the region bounded, or do I need behavior at infinity?
  • What symmetry is real, and what symmetry am I only hoping for?

What Laplace's equation is really saying

In a source-free region, the potential is harmonic. That means it does not have local spikes generated from inside the region. Whatever structure I see is being inherited from the boundary or from charges sitting somewhere else.

Practically, this is why conductor problems feel global: the answer at one point depends on the entire arrangement, not only on nearby geometry.

Uniqueness is the safety net

If I build a candidate potential that satisfies the governing equation in the physical region, obeys the boundary conditions, and has the right asymptotic behavior, then I am done. I do not need to “prove” it again by physical intuition.

This is the justification behind image charges and many Green-function constructions.

2. The Equations Worth Keeping Close

Free-Space Kernel, Delta Functions, and Green-Function Logic

Coulomb potential as the prototype

φ(r) = (1 / 4π ε0) ∫ ρ(r′) / |r - r′| d3r′

This is not just an integral formula to memorize. It is the free-space Green-function solution of Poisson's equation. The kernel 1 / |r - r′| is what space itself contributes before boundaries complicate the story.

Delta function identity I do not want to forget

∇2(1 / r) = -4π δ(3)(r)

This is the bridge between a singular point source and Poisson's equation. A point charge at r0 is represented by ρ(r) = q δ(3)(r - r0).

If I forget the Jacobian factors in curvilinear coordinates, the delta function stops doing its job correctly.

Green's function playbook

  • Pick G(r, r′) so that ∇2G = -4π δ(3)(r - r′) in the region.
  • Force G to satisfy the same boundary conditions as the physical problem.
  • Use the resulting integral representation to separate source terms from boundary terms.

Once the Green's function is adapted to the geometry, the rest of the problem becomes bookkeeping.

Quick checks for singular-source problems

  • Away from the source point, the potential should usually satisfy Laplace's equation.
  • The singularity should match the expected local behavior near the charge.
  • Total charge should come out correctly when integrating the source density.
  • Dimensions matter: δ(3) has units of inverse volume.

3. Canonical Problems

Plane, Sphere, and the Discipline of Boundary Conditions

Grounded plane with a point charge

Real charge q at (0, 0, a), image charge -q at (0, 0, -a). The candidate potential in the half-space z > 0 is

φ(r) = (q / 4π ε0) [ 1 / |r - r0| - 1 / |r - r0*| ]

On z = 0 the two distances match, so the boundary condition φ = 0 is automatic. At that point the uniqueness theorem finishes the argument.

Grounded sphere with an exterior charge

This is the version that feels more “Jackson” because the geometry is less forgiving and the algebra is more instructive.

q′ = -qR/a,    b = R2/a

The image charge sits inside the sphere, not in the physical exterior region. That is the whole point: image charges are mathematical devices, not claims about hidden matter inside the conductor.

Parallel-plate intuition

In a charge-free slab the potential is linear, not because “electric fields are always constant,” but because the geometry plus the boundary data leave only that harmonic option.

φ(x) = V0 x / d,    E = -(V0/d) x̂

This is the cleanest reminder that Laplace's equation can still produce a nontrivial field.

My method-of-images checklist

  • The images must lie outside the physical region.
  • The boundary condition has to be satisfied exactly, not approximately.
  • The potential must have the right singularities only where the real charges are.
  • The uniqueness theorem should be named explicitly in the solution.

4. Far-Field Structure and Matter

Multipoles, Dielectrics, and What Changes Once Material Responds

Multipole expansion in one sentence

When the observation point is far away from a localized source, the field can be organized by how fast each contribution falls with distance.

φ(r) = (1 / 4π ε0) [ Q / r + (p · r̂) / r2 + higher terms ]

The monopole term tells me whether any net charge survived. The dipole term tells me whether the first charge separation survived. Higher moments encode finer angular structure.

What I should hear when someone says “dielectric”

A dielectric does not kill the field the way a conductor does. Instead it responds by polarization, which introduces bound charge and changes the relationship between the source charge and the resulting field.

D = ε0E + P,    ∇ · D = ρfree

This separation between free and bound charge is the bookkeeping move that makes the subject manageable.

Boundary conditions I want in muscle memory

  • Tangential E is continuous across an electrostatic boundary.
  • The jump in the normal component of D is set by free surface charge.
  • At a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium, the interior field vanishes and the surface is equipotential.

Common failure mode

The easiest mistake is mixing up free charge, total charge, and bound charge, then applying the wrong boundary condition. If the answer for a dielectric interface looks too much like the vacuum answer, it probably is.

5. What To Remember Under Exam Pressure

A Short Working Summary and References

Five lines I would want on scratch paper

  • Electrostatics starts with E = -∇φ and ends with a boundary-value problem.
  • Poisson handles source regions; Laplace handles charge-free regions.
  • Uniqueness is what licenses clever constructions.
  • Green's functions package geometry plus boundary conditions into a kernel.
  • Multipoles and dielectrics are not separate worlds; they grow naturally out of the same framework.

Personal workflow for problem solving

  • Draw the physical region before touching the algebra.
  • Write the PDE and the boundary data on separate lines.
  • State symmetry honestly.
  • Check limiting behavior at the end.
  • If the answer is elegant, make sure uniqueness actually supports it.
